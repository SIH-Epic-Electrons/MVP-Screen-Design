1. Understanding Mule Accounts

Question
What exactly is a mule account? Does the attacker already have a list of mule accounts where they need to transfer money?
Answer

A mule account is a bank account used by criminals to receive and transfer stolen money, creating layers that make the money trail difficult to trace.

Key Insight: Yes, professional fraud gangs maintain networks of pre-arranged mule accounts. They recruit people (often poor/unemployed) to open accounts specifically for money laundering.

How criminals get mule accounts:

  • Recruitment: They recruit "money mules" - people who open accounts in their name for a small fee (â‚¹500-2000)
  • Buying accounts: They buy account details from people who need quick money
  • Identity theft: Using stolen KYC documents to open fake accounts
  • Compromised accounts: Taking over existing accounts through phishing

Question
Is there any connection between one mule account to another? How do they basically create a new mule account?
Answer

Yes, mule accounts are often interconnected in networks:

  • Same recruiter: One person might recruit 10-20 mules in their village/area
  • Geographic clusters: Accounts often opened in same region (Jharkhand's Jamtara is famous for this)
  • Similar patterns: Same branches, similar opening dates, same documents
  • Transaction patterns: Money flows between accounts in predictable ways
AEGIS Advantage: By using AI to detect these patterns (similar account opening times, geographic proximity, transaction flows), we can identify entire mule networks from one account!

How criminals create mule accounts:

  1. Find vulnerable people (students, unemployed, poor villagers)
  2. Offer â‚¹500-2000 to "rent" their Aadhaar/PAN for account opening
  3. Use digital KYC (video KYC) with the person present
  4. Get ATM card and mobile banking access
  5. Account is now ready to receive stolen money

  • NACH - Automated clearing house

6. Use Cases & Value Proposition

Question
Most people report fraud after 1-2 weeks. By then money is gone. Until you know it's fraud, you keep sending money. By the time you report to portal, money is gone. What is the actual use case?
Answer

You're asking the RIGHT question! AEGIS addresses this through multiple approaches:

Use Case 1: Early Reporters (Golden Hour)

Not everyone reports late. Some victims realize immediately:
  • Bank sends SMS for large transaction they didn't make
  • Family member notices and alerts quickly
  • Failed OTP entry alerts user something's wrong
For these early reporters, AEGIS enables instant freeze and potential arrest.

Use Case 2: Pattern-Based Prediction

AEGIS doesn't wait for complaints alone. It analyzes transaction patterns:
  • Middle-class account suddenly receives â‚¹5 lakhs
  • Immediately transfers to 3 different accounts
  • AI flags: "This looks like fraud - should we alert?"
We can potentially freeze BEFORE victim even knows!

Use Case 3: Late Report, Network Arrest

Even for late reports, identifying the mule network helps catch the gang:

  • One victim's report reveals 10 mule accounts
  • Those accounts are now flagged for all future transactions
  • When gang uses same network for next victim, we catch them

Use Case 4: Legitimate vs Fraud Scenario

You asked: "A middle class person needs money for operation/fees, suddenly large amount flows in, instantly transferred to bills. If money is blocked, it would be difficult for citizens, right?"

Answer: AEGIS uses multiple signals to differentiate:

Signal Legitimate Transaction Fraud Transaction
Source of money From known relative/employer From unknown account
Transfer pattern To known merchants (hospital, college) To unknown personal accounts
Account history Established account with history New account, no history
Geographic consistency Same city/area Different state withdrawals
Recipient pattern Verified merchant accounts Chain of personal accounts
Key Point: AEGIS doesn't auto-freeze legitimate transactions. It provides intelligence to officers who make the final call. The AI explains WHY it flagged something, allowing human judgment.

12. Anticipated Judge Questions

Tough Question
What if your AI makes a mistake and freezes an innocent person's account?
Answer Strategy
  • Human in the loop: AI recommends, officer approves. No auto-freeze without authorization.
  • Explainable AI: Every recommendation shows WHY - officer can validate before acting
  • Quick unfreeze: If mistake identified, unfreeze is equally fast
  • Confidence thresholds: Only high-confidence (>85%) cases get priority alerts
  • Legal framework: Follows existing CFCFRMS guidelines for temporary freezes
Tough Question
How will you get banks to share data? They're competitors!
Answer Strategy
  • Federated Learning: Banks don't share data - only model updates (mathematical gradients)
  • RBI mandate: Under fraud prevention, banks are required to cooperate
  • CFCFRMS precedent: This system already enables inter-bank cooperation
  • Mutual benefit: Every bank loses money to fraud - they WANT to cooperate
  • Privacy preserved: Customer data never leaves the bank's infrastructure
Tough Question
Criminals will adapt. Once they know about AEGIS, won't they change tactics?
Answer Strategy
  • Continuous learning: AEGIS learns from every case, including failed predictions
  • Adversarial training: Fraudster Mind Model simulates criminal adaptation
  • Economics still apply: Even if criminals adapt, friction increases. Slower cash-out = higher risk = less profit
  • Network effect: The more banks use AEGIS, the harder it is to find "safe" mule accounts anywhere
  • Arms race advantage: AI can adapt faster than human criminal networks
Tough Question
What's your accuracy rate? Can you guarantee results?
Answer Strategy
  • No system is 100%: We target 70%+ location prediction accuracy
  • Relative improvement: Current system: 0% prediction. Even 50% is infinitely better.
  • Continuous improvement: Every case (success or failure) improves the model
  • Multiple layers: Even if location prediction fails, account freeze still protects money
  • Measurable outcomes: Track recovery rate, not just prediction accuracy
